Statistics

Hayley routinely performs statistical analysis of environmental data to support site decision-making, including developing probability distributions for inputs used in performance and risk assessment models based on information in the literature and data collected at environmental sites. Ms. Brittingham has experience in a variety of multivariate statistical methods for both continuous and discrete data, including dimension reduction, shrinkage, classification, and a variety of GLMs. She has served as a statistical consultant for Pennsylvania State University, educating scientists in genetics and neuroscience on ethical ways to analyze their data.

Since September 2017, she has designed and reviewed sampling plans using a variety of sampling techniques, and analyzed environmental data for use in transparent and defensible decision making, reviewed statistical analysis for validity and defensibility as a statistician at Neptune and Company. In addition, she has provided statistical support for probabilistic performance assessments of radioactive waste disposal sites by developing distributions that consider the spatial and temporal complexities of the model and by performing sensitivity analyses to inform model implementation. Ecology and Evolution

  • Ashman Lab, Univeristy of Pittsburgh Hayley served as the primary contributor to a study that helped to clarify the effect of a plant’s chromosome counts on the extent of the area the species occupies.

  • Pruitt Lab, University of Pittsburgh. Hayley regularly assessed personality traits of social spiders (Agelenopsis and Stegodyphus). She also helped develop a novel assay for aggression on Stegodyphus, which were oversensitive to the usual procedure involving a metal rod. She performed various experiments on social ants and wasps regularly, and cared for all social spiders, ants, wasps, crickets and tarantulas by feeding and cleaning enclosures.

  • Swamp Sparrow Behavior, Smithsonian National Zoo. As an intern at the Smithsonian National Zoo, Hayley collected observational data on the rarely studied swamp sparrow, creating the first-ever ethogram (i.e., list of behaviors) and accompanying activity budget. She used non-parametric statistical methods to quantify relative dominance across the sample and found that birds with larger mass were more likely to be dominant after controlling for wing length, and further, that male sparrows with longer wings were generally dominant over those with shorter wings.

Hayley completed her Master’s with a 4.0 and her Bachelor’s cum laude. She was recognized for outstanding statistical methods by the American Society for Quality for her research on swamp sparrow dominance.
